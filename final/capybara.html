Here are some cool facts about capybaras:

Capybaras are the largest rodents on the planet, and they wear that title with a surprising amount of chill. Native to South America, these gentle giants can tip the scales at around 150 pounds and stretch out over four feet long—basically, they’re what you'd get if you scaled up a guinea pig and gave it a zen personality. Built for both land and water, capybaras are semi-aquatic by nature. With webbed feet and eyes set high on their heads, they can wade into a pond and hang out just under the surface, all while keeping watch like pros. Their diet is pretty straightforward—mostly grass and aquatic plants—but their digestive system is less conventional. To squeeze every bit of nutrition out of their greens, they practice something called autocoprophagy, which is a scientific way of saying they re-eat their own poop. Yeah. Efficient if nothing else. Capybaras aren’t loners either. They live in herds of 10 to 30, communicating with a whole catalog of sounds—chirps, barks, whistles, even purrs—each one serving a social purpose. What’s more impressive is how well they get along with others. Birds perch on them, monkeys hang around them, and even predators often seem to give them a pass. Their vibe is just that unbothered. In a twist of cultural logic, the Catholic Church once declared capybaras to be “fish,” making them fair game during Lent. And while that’s a bit odd, it speaks to how versatile and widespread these animals have become.
